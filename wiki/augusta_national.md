# Augusta National Golf Club

## Summary

Augusta National Golf Club is a private members-only golf club located in Augusta, Georgia, and is home to the [[Masters Tournament]], the first major championship of the calendar year. Founded in 1932 by legendary amateur golfer Bobby Jones and financier Clifford Roberts, the club was built on the grounds of a former indigo plantation and nursery — Fruitlands Nurseries — which is the reason the course's holes are named after trees, shrubs, and flowers. Augusta National is widely regarded as one of the most beautiful and strategically demanding golf courses in the world, and its immaculate conditioning, lush azalea blooms, and iconic white dogwoods have made it one of the most recognizable sporting venues on Earth.

## History and Design

Bobby Jones and Alister MacKenzie, the Scottish course architect responsible for Cypress Point and Royal Melbourne among others, designed Augusta National together. MacKenzie's genius was in creating holes that appeared wide and inviting from the tee but demanded precise second shots to correctly positioned areas of the green to enable birdie putts. Jones's vision was a course where each hole presented a clear problem but offered more than one solution — a course that would reward intelligent, thinking golf over brute force.

MacKenzie died in January 1934, just before the first Masters was held, and never saw the finished course played at the major level. Jones continued to oversee modifications to the course for decades. The course has been revised significantly over the years — most notably to add length in the 2000s in response to increasing driving distances, particularly by [[Tiger Woods]], whose dominance in 1997 prompted the club to "Tiger-proof" the layout.

## Course Layout and Signature Holes

Augusta National plays as a par 72 at approximately 7,545 yards for the Masters in recent years. The course is characterized by dramatic elevation changes, sweeping doglegs, and enormous, undulating greens that make putting one of the most demanding aspects of competition.

**Hole 12 — Golden Bell (par 3, 155 yards)**: The shortest hole on the course and arguably the most famous par 3 in golf. A narrow, shallow green is guarded by Rae's Creek in front and a bunker behind. Swirling winds through the trees make club selection nearly impossible to be certain of. It plays as the centerpiece of Amen Corner.

**Hole 13 — Azalea (par 5, 510 yards)**: A reachable par 5 curving left around Rae's Creek. Players who cut the corner aggressively with their tee shot may be able to reach the green in two, setting up eagle or birdie. Those who miss the green face a delicate chip or pitch over the creek. The risk-reward tension makes this one of the most thrilling holes in major championship golf.

**Hole 15 — Firethorn (par 5, 550 yards)**: Features a large pond fronting a tiered green. In 1935, Gene Sarazen holed his second shot from 235 yards for a double eagle (albatross) — the "shot heard 'round the world" — to force a playoff that he won. The pond continues to claim approach shots from players attempting to reach the green in two.

**Hole 16 — Redbud (par 3, 170 yards)**: Plays across a pond to a large green that funnels balls toward a center collection area. In 2005, [[Tiger Woods]] chipped in from the fringe to make birdie in one of the most watched moments in Masters history — his ball hovered on the edge of the cup for a moment before dropping in.

## Amen Corner (Holes 11, 12, 13)

Amen Corner — a term coined by writer Herbert Warren Wind after the 1958 Masters — refers to the stretch of holes 11, 12, and 13 where the tournament is frequently decided. The combination of Rae's Creek, the wind tunnel effect created by the surrounding trees, and the proximity of all three holes in the wooded lower portion of the course creates an arena-like atmosphere on Sunday afternoons. More Masters titles have been lost than won on these three holes.

## Membership and Traditions

Augusta National's membership is private, exclusive, and historically controversial. Women were not admitted as members until 2012, when former U.S. Secretary of State Condoleezza Rice and financier Darla Moore became the club's first female members. The club does not disclose its membership list, and the waiting list for membership is by invitation only.

The club's "patrons" (it prohibits the use of the word "spectators") experience one of sport's most controlled and immersive environments: no advertising, no corporate signage, and food prices kept deliberately low as a matter of tradition.
