# PGA Championship

## Summary

The PGA Championship is the fourth and final major championship of the calendar year (typically held in May since 2019), organized by the Professional Golfers' Association of America. Founded in 1916, the championship awards the Wanamaker Trophy — the largest and heaviest of the four major trophies — and has historically served to honor and elevate the professional game. It began as a match play event and retains some of that aggressive spirit even in its current stroke play format. The PGA Championship is contested at some of the most rigorous private and public courses in the United States and has produced an array of iconic champions.

## The Wanamaker Trophy

The Wanamaker Trophy is named after Rodman Wanamaker, the department store magnate who hosted the organizational meeting in January 1916 that founded the PGA of America. Wanamaker funded the early prize purse and the purchase of the trophy. At 28 inches tall and nearly 30 pounds, it is the largest of the four major trophies and must be carried by two hands. Champions receive a replica; the original tours golf exhibitions and is kept under PGA of America custody.

Jim Barnes won the inaugural PGA Championship in 1916, and the trophy was not awarded during 1917-1918 due to World War I, or in 1943 due to World War II.

## Match Play Era (1916–1957)

For its first four decades, the PGA Championship was decided by match play — a format in which golfers compete hole by hole rather than by total stroke count. This format produced some of the era's most intense competition, with Walter Hagen winning a record five PGA Championships (1921, 1924, 1925, 1926, 1927) using his famous psychological tactics and match play brilliance. Gene Sarazen, Sam Snead, and Byron Nelson also thrived in the match play format.

The switch to stroke play in 1958 was controversial among traditionalists but was made for television and logistical reasons. Some argue the match play format suited the PGA Championship's underdog-friendly reputation, as an off day could not sink a competitor the way it can in stroke play.

## Notable Venues

- **Baltusrol Golf Club** (Springfield, New Jersey): Has hosted the PGA Championship twice, most recently in 2016 when Jimmy Walker won.
- **Valhalla Golf Club** (Louisville, Kentucky): A regular stop on the major rotation; [[Rory McIlroy]] won his second PGA Championship here in 2014.
- **Bethpage Black** (Farmingdale, New York): A public course hosting the PGA Championship in 2019, when Brooks Koepka won his second consecutive PGA title.
- **Kiawah Island Ocean Course** (South Carolina): One of the most exposed, wind-lashed courses in America; Phil Mickelson won his first major here in 2021 at age 50, becoming the oldest major champion in history.
- **Oak Hill Country Club** (Rochester, New York): Hosted the 2023 PGA Championship, won by Koepka.

## Major Champions and Records

- **Walter Hagen**: Five PGA Championship victories — the most of any player.
- **Jack Nicklaus**: Won five PGA Championships (1963, 1971, 1973, 1975, 1980), a record he shares with Hagen.
- **[[Tiger Woods]]**: Won four PGA Championships (1999, 2000, 2006, 2007), tied for the most in the stroke play era.
- **Brooks Koepka**: Won four PGA Championships (2018, 2019, 2023, 2025), demonstrating a rare aptitude for the championship's demanding setups.
- **Phil Mickelson**: Won in 2021 at Kiawah Island, making him the oldest major champion in the history of men's professional golf at 50 years, 11 months.
- **[[Rory McIlroy]]**: Won the PGA Championship in 2012 and 2014 as part of his four major victories.

## The PGA of America vs. PGA Tour

A source of frequent confusion is the distinction between the PGA of America (which runs the PGA Championship, the Ryder Cup when played on American soil, and club professional programs) and the PGA Tour (the separate organization governing the primary North American professional tour). The two organizations are legally and operationally independent, a split that became formal in 1968 when tour pros broke away to form their own organization.
