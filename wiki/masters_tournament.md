# Masters Tournament

## Summary

The Masters Tournament is the first of golf's four major championships played each calendar year, held annually in April at [[Augusta National Golf Club]] in Augusta, Georgia. Founded by Bobby Jones and Clifford Roberts in 1934, the Masters is unique among majors for returning to the same course every year, allowing the course and its traditions to become as celebrated as the competition itself. The tournament is renowned for its immaculate presentation, its invitation-only field, the iconic green jacket awarded to champions, and the dramatic back-nine on Sunday that has produced some of the most memorable moments in sports history.

## History and Founding

Bobby Jones, who retired from competitive golf in 1930 after completing the Grand Slam (winning all four major titles then in existence in a single year), co-founded [[Augusta National Golf Club]] in 1932 with financier Clifford Roberts. The club's course was designed by Jones and legendary architect Alister MacKenzie on the grounds of a former indigo plantation and nursery. The first Masters was held in 1933, with Horton Smith winning the inaugural event.

The tournament was not officially called "The Masters" until 1939 — Jones initially resisted the grandiose name. It was not played from 1943 to 1945 due to World War II, and the grounds were used for livestock grazing during that period.

## The Green Jacket Tradition

The green jacket is the most iconic symbol in golf. Augusta National members have worn green jackets since 1937 to make themselves identifiable to patrons. The tradition of awarding the jacket to the champion began in 1949, when Sam Snead received the first champion's jacket. Each winner has their measurements taken and a jacket made for them; the jacket must remain at Augusta National and is worn by the champion at the following year's ceremony when they place it on the new champion.

Past champions kept in the Augusta National clubhouse's Champions Locker Room include jackets belonging to [[Tiger Woods]] (five-time champion: 1997, 2001, 2002, 2005, 2019), Jack Nicklaus (six-time champion), and [[Rory McIlroy]], who completed his career Grand Slam at Augusta.

## Amen Corner

The term "Amen Corner" was coined by writer Herbert Warren Wind in 1958 to describe the stretch of holes 11, 12, and 13 — arguably the most dramatic and dangerous sequence of holes in major championship golf. The name references the spiritual stakes that seem to hang over every shot played there.

- **Hole 11 (White Dogwood, par 4)**: A long dogleg left with a pond guarding the left side of the green, demanding a precise approach.
- **Hole 12 (Golden Bell, par 3)**: The shortest hole at Augusta at just 155 yards, but notoriously treacherous due to swirling, unpredictable winds that funnel through the trees. Rae's Creek fronts the green; a thin strip of bunker sits behind. Many tournament hopes have been dashed here.
- **Hole 13 (Azalea, par 5)**: A reachable par 5 curving around Rae's Creek, rewarding bold players who successfully cut the corner with their tee shot.

## Notable Champions and Moments

- **Gene Sarazen (1935)**: Made a double eagle (albatross) on the 15th hole in the final round to force a playoff, which he won. It remains one of the most famous shots in golf history.
- **Jack Nicklaus (1986)**: At age 46, the Golden Bear shot a closing 65 to win his sixth Masters title — widely regarded as one of the greatest final rounds in golf history.
- **Tiger Woods (1997)**: Won his first Masters by 12 strokes at age 21, shattering the tournament scoring record with 270 (18 under par).
- **Tiger Woods (2019)**: Won his fifth Masters after an 11-year major drought following career-threatening back surgeries, completing one of sport's greatest comebacks.
- **Phil Mickelson**: Won three Masters titles (2004, 2006, 2010), cementing his status as one of the tournament's all-time greats.
