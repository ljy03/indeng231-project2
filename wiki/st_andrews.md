# St Andrews Links

## Summary

St Andrews Links, located in the coastal town of St Andrews in Fife, Scotland, is universally recognized as the "home of golf" and is the most historically significant golf venue in the world. The Old Course at St Andrews has been played continuously since at least the 15th century, making it one of the oldest sports venues in human history. The Links is a public facility — any member of the public can pay to play the Old Course through a ballot system — and has hosted [[The Open Championship]] more times than any other course. Its fairways, bunkers, double greens, and the iconic Swilcan Bridge are as familiar to golfers worldwide as any image in the sport.

## History as the Home of Golf

Golf has been played at St Andrews since the 15th century, and the town's association with the game is so deep that it has shaped the sport's fundamental structure. It was at St Andrews that golfers first settled on 18 holes as the standard round in 1764, by reducing the existing 22-hole course. The Royal and Ancient Golf Club of St Andrews (R&A), founded in 1754, became the game's primary rule-making body alongside the USGA, governing golf worldwide until the two organizations formally assumed joint responsibility for the rules in 2004.

The town itself is centered on golf in a way almost no other place on Earth is. The Old Course runs through the heart of St Andrews, with the 18th hole's fairway shared with the 1st, and the broad expanse of the course visible from the town center.

## The Old Course Layout

The Old Course is unique among links layouts for several reasons. It is played out and back along a narrow peninsula of land, meaning the outward nine holes share fairways and double greens with the inward nine. Double greens — enormous shared putting surfaces serving two different holes simultaneously — are one of the Old Course's most distinctive features; seven double greens exist, with the 5th and 13th sharing the largest putting surface in the world.

The course plays to a par of 72 at approximately 7,310 yards. Wind direction is decisive, as the course can play out or back depending on the breeze, making local knowledge and experience invaluable.

## The Road Hole (17th)

The Road Hole — the par-4 17th — is widely described as the most famous and difficult par 4 in golf. Players must drive over or around the corner of a replica of the Old Course Hotel (which obscures the view of the fairway), leaving a long approach to a narrow green bounded on the right by the "Road Hole Bunker" — an impossibly deep pot bunker — and on the left by an asphalt road and stone wall that are in play.

A ball in the Road Hole Bunker often cannot be advanced directly toward the pin; many players are forced to chip or putt out sideways to avoid compounding the problem. The road and wall behind the green are part of the course — there is no out of bounds — meaning balls can run off the green onto the road and players must play them from there.

## The Swilcan Bridge

The Swilcan Bridge is a small stone bridge crossing the Swilcan Burn (stream) on the 18th fairway, estimated to be over 700 years old. It is wide enough to cross comfortably but serves no practical purpose for golfers — the stream is easily stepped over. Its significance is purely symbolic: it is the bridge every champion and every pilgrim to St Andrews walks across on the 18th fairway.

The moment when a great player known to be playing their final Open Championship pauses on the Swilcan Bridge to acknowledge the crowd has become one of golf's most emotional rituals. Jack Nicklaus's farewell wave on the bridge at the 2005 Open brought the gallery — and much of the watching world — to tears.

## The Open Championship at St Andrews

St Andrews has hosted [[The Open Championship]] 29 times, more than any other course. Notable Opens there include:

- **2000**: [[Tiger Woods]] won by eight strokes in one of the most dominant major victories in history, notably never finding a bunker in four rounds.
- **2005**: Woods again, in an emotional championship that included Nicklaus's farewell on the Swilcan Bridge.
- **2010**: Louis Oosthuizen of South Africa won by seven strokes.
- **2022**: [[Rory McIlroy]] challenged but Cameron Smith of Australia won with a stunning final-round 64.
