# The Open Championship

## Summary

The Open Championship — commonly called "The Open" in golf tradition, or "the British Open" in American usage — is the oldest golf major in the world, first played in 1860 at Prestwick Golf Club in Scotland. Organized by The R&A, it is played on links courses along the coastlines of Great Britain and Ireland, where the game was born. The champion receives the Claret Jug, one of sport's most historic trophies, and the title of "Champion Golfer of the Year." The Open's links conditions — firm, fast fairways, unpredictable winds, pot bunkers, and no water hazards — demand a style of golf fundamentally different from any other major.

## Links Golf and Course Conditions

Links golf is played on the type of terrain for which the game was originally invented: sandy, undulating ground between the sea and farmable land, covered in fescue grasses, with few or no trees. The ground is typically firm and fast, meaning that a bump-and-run approach — running the ball along the ground to the green — is often more effective than the aerial attack employed on parkland courses.

Wind is the defining challenge of links golf. Because there are virtually no trees to shelter players, gusts can shift direction and speed unpredictably, turning a straightforward hole into a strategic puzzle with no clear answer. Players who excel at keeping the ball low, controlling trajectory, and adapting club selection mid-round have a significant advantage.

Pot bunkers — small, steep-sided sand traps cut into the earth — can be penal rather than merely penalizing. At [[St Andrews Links]], the Road Hole bunker on the 17th is so deep and steeply faced that players are sometimes forced to putt or chip out sideways rather than attempt to advance the ball toward the hole.

## The Claret Jug

The Claret Jug (formally the Golf Champion Trophy) has been awarded since 1873. The original trophy was a challenge belt of red Morocco leather, given to the winner from 1860 to 1870, before the Claret Jug was commissioned after Tom Morris Jr. won three consecutive championships and was allowed to keep the belt. Each champion's name is engraved on the Jug, which is on display at the R&A museum in St Andrews between championships. The winner receives a replica to keep.

## Rotation of Venues

The Open rotates among a select group of links courses, all in Great Britain:

- **[[St Andrews Links]] (Old Course)**: The "home of golf," having hosted The Open 29 times — more than any other venue. It is considered a pilgrimage site for golfers worldwide.
- **Royal Birkdale** (Southport, England): Known for its fair but demanding challenge; [[Tiger Woods]] won his third Open here in 2006.
- **Royal St George's** (Sandwich, England): The only English course south of Manchester on the Open rota; Darren Clarke won here in 2011.
- **Muirfield** (Gullane, Scotland): Regarded as among the fairest links tests; Phil Mickelson won here in 2013.
- **Royal Troon** (Ayrshire, Scotland): Home of the "Postage Stamp" — the famous par-3 8th hole — and scene of [[Rory McIlroy]]'s dramatic playoff victory in 2025.
- **Royal Portrush** (Northern Ireland): Hosted The Open in 1951 and returned in 2019 for the first time in 68 years.

## Memorable Moments

- **Tom Watson (1977, Turnberry)**: Watson and Jack Nicklaus played two of the greatest rounds in major history side by side in the "Duel in the Sun," with Watson prevailing by one stroke.
- **Jean Van de Velde (1999, Carnoustie)**: Leading by three strokes on the 72nd hole, the French journeyman made a catastrophic triple bogey, losing in a playoff to Paul Lawrie in one of golf's most stunning collapses.
- **Tiger Woods (2000, St Andrews)**: Won by eight strokes without once finding a bunker in four rounds — perhaps the most dominant major victory of the modern era.
- **Phil Mickelson (2013, Muirfield)**: Shot a stunning final-round 66 to win his first and only Open Championship, cementing his career Grand Slam.
