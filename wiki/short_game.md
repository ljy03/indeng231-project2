# Short Game

## Summary

The short game encompasses all shots played from close range to the hole, typically within 100 yards — including chipping, pitching, bunker play, and putting. While the [[Golf Swing|full swing]] attracts most attention for its power and athleticism, the short game is where the majority of strokes are saved or lost. Statistics consistently show that approximately 60–65% of all strokes in a round of golf occur within 100 yards of the hole, making short game proficiency the single most important factor separating mid-handicap players from elite amateurs and professionals. Legendary teacher Harvey Penick summarized it simply: "The woods are full of long hitters."

## Chipping

Chipping is a low, running shot played from just off the green when the ball lies in the fringe or light rough and there is a significant amount of green between the ball and the hole. The goal is to land the ball on the green quickly and let it roll out to the hole like a putt, minimizing the time the ball spends in the air (where spin, wind, and trajectory make it harder to control).

A standard chip uses a 7- or 8-iron, pitching wedge, or gap wedge — the choice depends on how much room exists between the ball and the edge of the green and how far the ball must travel to the hole. The chipping motion is essentially a putting stroke with a lofted club: a firm-wristed rocking of the shoulders with minimal lower body movement. Weight is positioned slightly toward the lead foot at address, and the hands lead the clubhead through impact, producing a descending strike that keeps the ball low.

## Pitching

Pitching involves higher, shorter shots that carry more of the distance in the air and land softly with greater spin, used when obstacles (rough, bunkers, water) must be carried or when a precise distance control to a specific spot on the green is needed. A pitch shot uses a gap wedge, sand wedge, or lob wedge and requires more wrist hinge and body rotation than a chip.

Distance control in pitching is governed by the length and speed of the backswing rather than by changing the pace of the downswing (which causes inconsistency). Tour professionals practice specific backswing lengths — often using a clock-face system, referencing how far the left arm swings back (9 o'clock, 10 o'clock, etc.) — to produce precise yardages with each wedge.

The ability to control trajectory is also crucial: in windy conditions, a lower "punch" pitch keeps the ball under the wind and provides more predictable distance; in calm conditions with a tight pin, a high lob wedge shot can be used to land the ball softly with minimal roll.

## Bunker Play

Greenside bunker shots are among the most feared shots in golf for amateurs but are considered routine by professionals. The correct technique for a standard bunker shot involves striking the sand approximately two inches behind the ball, allowing the club's bounce (the angle on the sole of the sand wedge) to slide through the sand and propel the ball out on a cushion of displaced sand — the ball is never actually struck directly.

Key setup adjustments for bunker play: open the clubface significantly (pointing right of the target for a right-handed player), take a wide open stance aimed left, and swing along the body line (left) while the open face aims the ball at the target. The depth of the divot in the sand controls both distance and trajectory. Wet, packed sand requires less bounce and a shallower entry angle; soft, fluffy sand demands more aggressive swing speed and a deeper divot.

## Putting

Putting typically accounts for approximately 35–40% of all strokes in a round of golf, making it the single most important discipline in scoring. A professional player averages around 29 putts per round on the PGA Tour; reducing putts by even two or three per round has a dramatic scoring impact.

Effective putting requires three elements: accurate read (understanding how the slope and grain of the green will influence the ball's path), precise start line (the putter face must be square to the intended start line at impact), and correct pace (the right speed so the ball arrives at the hole with enough energy to go in but not so much that a missed putt races past).

Green reading involves crouching behind the ball to observe the overall contour, reading the break from all sides of the hole, and factoring in grain direction (which way the grass grows affects the ball's path). On courses like [[Augusta National Golf Club]], the complexity of the multi-tiered, steeply sloping greens makes green reading as important as the physical act of putting.

## Importance for Scoring

Professionals spend the majority of their practice time on the short game despite it requiring the least athletic ability. For amateur golfers pursuing lower scores, the return on investment from short game practice is far greater than from working on the full swing. A player who consistently gets up-and-down from difficult lies around the green — completing a chip and a single putt — saves far more strokes over a round than any swing improvement.
