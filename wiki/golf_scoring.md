# Golf Scoring & Terminology

## Summary

Golf uses a distinctive scoring system built around the concept of par — the expected number of strokes a skilled golfer should need to complete a hole or an entire round. Scores are expressed in relation to par, using a rich vocabulary of bird-themed terms for under-par scores and simple numerical terms for over-par results. Understanding golf scoring also requires familiarity with formats like stroke play and match play, as well as the handicap system that allows players of different abilities to compete fairly against one another.

## Par and Score Terminology

Par is the baseline score for each hole, set based on the hole's length and difficulty. Par-3 holes are typically under 250 yards; par-4 holes range from roughly 250 to 470 yards; par-5 holes extend beyond that, with some modern championship holes exceeding 600 yards. Most 18-hole courses have a total par between 70 and 72.

Scores relative to par on a single hole are named as follows:

- **Condor**: 4 under par (extremely rare; essentially only possible with a hole-in-one on a par 5)
- **Albatross (Double Eagle)**: 3 under par — most famously made by Gene Sarazen with his "shot heard 'round the world" at the 1935 [[Masters Tournament]]
- **Eagle**: 2 under par — common on par 5s for professionals
- **Birdie**: 1 under par — the standard target for professionals on most holes
- **Par**: Even — the expected score
- **Bogey**: 1 over par
- **Double Bogey**: 2 over par
- **Triple Bogey**: 3 over par

## Stroke Play

Stroke play is the most common format in professional and amateur competition. Each shot counts as one stroke, and the player's total strokes over the round (or tournament) determine the winner. Most professional events are 72-hole stroke play tournaments, with cuts made after 36 or 54 holes to reduce the field to the top finishers. The [[Masters Tournament]], [[U.S. Open Golf]], [[The Open Championship]], and [[PGA Championship]] are all contested under stroke play.

Scores in multi-round stroke play events are tracked cumulatively. A player shooting 67-68-72-65 over four rounds would have a total of 272, which would be expressed as the sum relative to par (e.g., 16 under par on a par-72 course).

## Match Play

Match play scores holes rather than total strokes. On each hole, the player who takes fewer strokes wins the hole; if both players take the same number, the hole is halved. A player wins the match when they lead by more holes than remain to be played — for example, being "4 up with 3 to play" is described as winning 4&3.

Match play encourages aggressive, attacking golf because a bad hole costs only one hole rather than several strokes. The [[PGA Championship]] was conducted as match play from its inception in 1916 until 1958, when it switched to stroke play. The Ryder Cup and Presidents Cup team competitions use match play formats.

## Handicap System

Golf's [[Golf Handicap System|handicap system]] allows players of different abilities to compete on equal terms. A player's handicap index represents their demonstrated ability, calculated from recent scores. When playing a course, the handicap index is adjusted by the course's slope and rating to produce a course handicap, which determines how many strokes of advantage a player receives.

A scratch golfer plays to a handicap of zero. A 10-handicapper would receive 10 strokes distributed across the 18 holes during a round against a scratch player. The World Handicap System, adopted globally in 2020, standardized calculation methods across all national associations, replacing several inconsistent regional systems.

## Stableford and Other Formats

Stableford scoring awards points based on score relative to par on each hole: typically 1 point for bogey, 2 for par, 3 for birdie, 4 for eagle. The player with the most points wins. This format rewards aggressive play while limiting the damage of a disastrous hole. The International, played on the PGA Tour, used a modified Stableford format.

Other formats include four-ball (better ball), foursomes (alternate shot), scramble, and skins — each creating different strategic dynamics and team structures.
