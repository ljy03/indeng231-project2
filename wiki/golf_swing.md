# Golf Swing

## Summary

The golf swing is the coordinated sequence of body movements used to strike a golf ball with a club. It is one of the most technically demanding athletic motions in sport, requiring precise coordination of the hands, arms, shoulders, hips, and legs across a full-body rotational movement that can take less than two seconds from start to finish. While swing styles vary widely among professionals, all effective swings share fundamental principles of grip, stance, rotation, and impact position that determine ball flight, direction, and distance.

## Grip

The grip is the golfer's only physical connection to the club, making it the foundation of the entire swing. There are three primary grip styles: the overlapping (Vardon) grip, in which the pinky of the trailing hand rests on top of the index finger of the lead hand; the interlocking grip, where those same fingers intertwine; and the ten-finger (baseball) grip, in which all fingers contact the club. Most professional players use the overlap or interlock.

Grip pressure should be firm enough to control the club but relaxed enough to allow wrist hinge and natural rotation. Gripping too tightly creates tension in the forearms and restricts the swing arc. Hand position on the grip — neutral, strong, or weak — influences face angle at impact and the resulting ball flight shape.

## Stance and Setup

A proper setup provides the stable foundation from which the swing is built. Feet are positioned roughly shoulder-width apart for mid-irons, slightly wider for the driver, and narrower for short irons and wedges. Weight is distributed evenly between both feet at address, with a slight flex in the knees and a forward tilt from the hips — not the waist — to establish the swing plane.

Ball position varies by club: forward in the stance (near the lead heel) for the driver, and progressively more central as clubs get shorter. The spine angle established at address should be maintained throughout the swing. Alignment of feet, hips, and shoulders parallel to the target line is essential for directional accuracy.

## Backswing

The backswing begins with a one-piece takeaway — the hands, arms, and shoulders moving together as a unit — rotating the upper body over a relatively stable lower body. The club travels back along the swing plane as the lead shoulder turns under the chin. At the top of the backswing, the lead arm should be relatively straight, the wrists hinged to approximately 90 degrees, and the club shaft ideally parallel to the ground and pointing toward the target.

Hip rotation during the backswing is intentionally limited compared to shoulder rotation, creating a torque differential — often called the X-factor — between the upper and lower body. This coiled tension is the primary source of power in the swing.

## Downswing and Impact

The downswing is initiated not with the arms but with the lower body — specifically the hips rotating toward the target and the lead leg driving into the ground. This sequential "kinetic chain" transfers energy from the ground up through the legs, hips, torso, arms, and finally the clubhead. The club drops into the correct swing plane as the hips clear.

Impact is the moment of truth. At impact, the hands should be slightly ahead of the ball (shaft lean forward), the hips open to the target, and the clubface square. For iron shots, this forward shaft lean produces a slightly descending blow that compresses the ball against the turf, generating backspin and control. For driver shots off a tee, a slight upward attack angle maximizes distance.

## Follow-Through and Finish

After impact, the momentum of the swing continues into the follow-through. The arms extend fully down the target line before rotating over. A complete, balanced finish — weight fully on the lead foot, chest facing the target, the club resting behind the lead shoulder — indicates that the swing has been executed with proper sequencing and rotation. Players like [[Tiger Woods]] and [[Rory McIlroy]] are known for their powerfully balanced finish positions.
