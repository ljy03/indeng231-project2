# Rules of Golf

## Summary

The Rules of Golf are the official regulations governing the sport, jointly maintained by the R&A (based in St Andrews, Scotland) and the United States Golf Association (USGA). First codified in 1744 at Leith, Scotland, the rules have evolved over centuries into a comprehensive framework covering everything from how to tee off to how to handle unplayable lies and equipment standards. A major revision in 2019 simplified many rules and modernized the language, reducing the total number of rules from 34 to 24 while introducing player-friendly updates such as dropping from knee height instead of shoulder height.

## Out of Bounds and Lost Ball

Out of bounds (OB) is defined by white stakes or lines marking the boundary of the course. A ball struck out of bounds must be replayed from the original spot with a one-stroke penalty — resulting in stroke-and-distance, effectively costing the player two strokes. A ball is considered lost if it is not found within three minutes of beginning the search.

The 2019 rules introduced a Local Rule option allowing recreational players to drop in the vicinity of where the ball went out of bounds with a two-stroke penalty, avoiding the time-consuming walk back to the previous spot. This option is not permitted in professional tournament play, where stroke-and-distance remains mandatory.

## Penalty Areas (Water Hazards)

Penalty areas — marked by red or yellow stakes — replace what were historically called water hazards. Yellow-staked penalty areas offer two relief options: play from the original spot under stroke-and-distance, or drop behind the penalty area keeping the point where the ball crossed the margin on a line with the flag. Red-staked penalty areas add a third option: lateral relief, allowing the player to drop within two club lengths of where the ball entered the hazard, no closer to the hole.

Famous penalty areas include the pond fronting the 15th and 16th holes at [[Augusta National Golf Club]], which has swallowed countless balls during the [[Masters Tournament]].

## Order of Play and Teeing Ground Rules

On the teeing ground, the player with the lowest score on the previous hole has the "honor" and tees off first. In stroke play, playing out of order carries no penalty, though players are encouraged to follow proper sequence. In match play, playing out of turn may allow the opponent to require the stroke be cancelled and replayed.

The ball must be teed between the tee markers and within two club lengths behind them. Teeing a ball in front of the markers incurs a two-stroke penalty in stroke play. Players may tee the ball on a wooden or plastic tee peg, or directly on the ground.

## Unplayable Lies and Drop Procedures

A player may declare their ball unplayable anywhere on the course except in a penalty area, accepting a one-stroke penalty and choosing from three relief options: stroke-and-distance back to where they last played; dropping within two club lengths of the original spot, no closer to the hole; or dropping behind the original spot, keeping it between the drop point and the hole.

Under the 2019 rules, drops must be taken from knee height (not shoulder height) and must land and come to rest in the defined relief area. If the ball rolls outside the relief area, it is re-dropped; after two failed re-drops, it is placed.

## Etiquette and Spirit of the Game

Golf is unique among major sports in that players are expected to call penalties on themselves. This self-policing tradition reflects the sport's deep commitment to honesty and integrity. Key etiquette includes: repairing ball marks on greens, raking bunkers after play, not walking on another player's putting line, keeping pace with the group ahead, and maintaining silence during another player's shot.

The concept of the "spirit of the game" was formally incorporated into the 2019 rules revision, recognizing that most golfers play without a referee and must act fairly at all times. Slow play is penalized in professional competition, with shot clocks introduced on some tours to enforce pace-of-play standards.
