# Golf Clubs & Equipment

## Summary

Golf equipment encompasses the clubs, balls, bags, and accessories used to play the game. The Rules of Golf permit a maximum of 14 clubs per round, and players typically assemble a set that covers the full range of distances and shot types they will face on the course. Equipment technology has advanced dramatically over the past several decades, with modern clubs featuring materials and engineering that were unimaginable in the sport's early history. Despite this evolution, skill, strategy, and course management remain far more important than equipment in determining a player's score.

## The Driver and Fairway Woods

The driver is the longest club in the bag, designed to hit the ball maximum distances off the tee. Modern drivers feature large titanium or carbon fiber heads (up to the maximum permitted 460cc volume), thin clubfaces that act like a trampoline at impact, and adjustable hosels that allow players to modify loft and face angle. Elite professionals generate clubhead speeds exceeding 120 mph with the driver, producing carry distances of 300+ yards.

Fairway woods (typically 3-wood and 5-wood) are versatile clubs used for long shots from the fairway or for tee shots on tight par-4 holes where accuracy is prioritized over distance. Hybrids — a crossbreed between fairway woods and long irons — became popular in the early 2000s and have largely replaced 3- and 4-irons in most recreational and many professional bags, offering easier launch from difficult lies.

## Irons

Irons are numbered from 1 to 9, with lower numbers producing lower trajectory and greater distance, and higher numbers producing more loft, spin, and shorter distance. A typical set carried by recreational players includes 5-iron through 9-iron plus the pitching wedge; better players often carry 4-iron or even 3-iron. Tour professionals frequently carry 4-iron through pitching wedge, relying on their ability to generate sufficient clubhead speed to launch long irons effectively.

Iron heads are either cast (hollow, cavity-back designs that distribute weight around the perimeter for forgiveness) or forged (a solid, dense construction preferred by better players for feel and workability). Forged irons require more precise ball-striking but reward skilled players with superior feedback and the ability to shape shots.

## Wedges

Wedges are the highest-lofted clubs in the bag, used for approach shots from within 130 yards, chip shots, pitch shots, and bunker play. Modern players typically carry three or four wedges:

- **Pitching Wedge (PW)**: 44–48 degrees of loft; typically part of the iron set
- **Gap Wedge (GW)**: 50–52 degrees; fills the gap between pitching wedge and sand wedge
- **Sand Wedge (SW)**: 54–58 degrees; designed with a wide "bounce" sole that prevents the club from digging in sand
- **Lob Wedge (LW)**: 58–64 degrees; used for high, soft shots around the green and from difficult lies

Wedge grooves are regulated by the USGA and R&A; sharp grooves grip the ball at impact and generate backspin that helps approach shots stop quickly on greens.

## The Putter

The putter is used to roll the ball along the green into the hole and is typically the most-used club in any round of golf — putting accounts for roughly 40% of all strokes. Putters come in three primary head styles: blade putters (traditional, compact), mallet putters (larger, higher moment of inertia), and face-balanced or toe-hang variations that suit different putting strokes.

Putter length is regulated (minimum 18 inches) and conventional putter lengths range from 32 to 36 inches. Belly and long (broomhandle) putters, once popular on tour, were effectively banned in 2016 when the Rules of Golf prohibited anchoring the club to the body during a stroke.

## Shaft Flex and Ball Construction

Shaft flex — how much a shaft bends during the swing — affects trajectory and shot shape. Shafts are rated from L (Ladies), A (Senior), R (Regular), S (Stiff), to X (Extra Stiff). Faster swingers require stiffer shafts to control ball flight; fitting to the appropriate flex is one of the most impactful equipment decisions a golfer can make.

Modern golf balls feature multilayer construction: a soft urethane cover surrounds one or more layers of different materials engineered to maximize both spin control around the green and distance off the tee. Two-piece balls (hard Surlyn cover, minimal spin) are designed for recreational players prioritizing distance; three- and four-piece balls (urethane cover) are preferred by professionals and skilled amateurs for their spin control and feel on short shots, central to a strong [[Short Game]].
