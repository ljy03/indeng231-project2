# Tiger Woods

## Summary

Eldrick Tont "Tiger" Woods (born December 30, 1975, in Cypress, California) is widely regarded as one of the greatest golfers of all time and one of the most influential athletes in sports history. He has won 15 major championships — second only to Jack Nicklaus's 18 — and held the world number-one ranking for a record 683 weeks during his career. Woods transformed professional golf, dramatically increasing television ratings, prize money, and the sport's global following through an unprecedented combination of athletic power, mental toughness, and relentless competitiveness. His career has encompassed both transcendent peaks and devastating personal and physical lows, yet his 2019 [[Masters Tournament]] victory — following multiple back surgeries and years of injury — stands as one of sport's greatest comeback stories.

## Early Life and Amateur Career

Tiger Woods was introduced to golf by his father, Earl Woods, at an extraordinarily young age — he appeared on The Mike Douglas Show putting at age two. He was a child prodigy who won the Optimist International Junior World Golf Championships six times, and he became the youngest U.S. Junior Amateur champion at age 15. He won three consecutive U.S. Amateur championships (1994, 1995, 1996) — a feat never accomplished before or since — before turning professional in August 1996 at age 20.

## Professional Dominance

Woods won his first major, the [[Masters Tournament]], in April 1997 by 12 strokes with a then-record score of 270 (18 under par). He was 21 years old. He went on to hold all four major championship titles simultaneously from the 2000 [[U.S. Open Golf]] through the 2001 Masters — a feat journalists called the "Tiger Slam," as it was not a calendar-year Grand Slam but represented holding all four at once.

His 2000 season is considered by many the greatest in professional golf history: he won three majors (the U.S. Open by 15 strokes at Pebble Beach, [[The Open Championship]] by eight strokes at [[St Andrews Links]], and the [[PGA Championship]]) and was the PGA Tour's leading money winner. He won 79 official PGA Tour events in his career, second only to Sam Snead's 82.

## The 15 Majors

Woods's major championships:
- **Masters Tournament**: 1997, 2001, 2002, 2005, 2019
- **U.S. Open**: 2000, 2002, 2008
- **The Open Championship**: 2000, 2005, 2006
- **PGA Championship**: 1999, 2000, 2006, 2007

His 2008 U.S. Open victory at Torrey Pines — won in a Monday playoff against Rocco Mediate while playing on a broken leg and torn anterior cruciate ligament — is considered among the most extraordinary individual performances in sports history.

## Injuries and Comeback

After 2008, Woods underwent multiple knee and back surgeries that cost him extended periods of competitive golf. He underwent four back surgeries between 2014 and 2017, with spinal fusion surgery in April 2017 leaving many to question whether he would ever compete seriously again. His return to form culminated in his victory at the 2019 Masters — his first major in 11 years — which is broadly described as the greatest comeback in golf history.

In February 2021, Woods was seriously injured in a single-car rollover accident in Rancho Palos Verdes, California. He suffered severe injuries to his right leg, including comminuted open fractures to the tibia and fibula, and required hours of surgery. Despite the severity of the injuries, he returned to competitive play at the 2022 Masters, completing all four rounds.

## Cultural Impact

Beyond statistics, Tiger Woods fundamentally changed the demographics and economics of golf. Before Woods, the sport was perceived in the United States as predominantly white and upper-class. As a mixed-race athlete of African American, Thai, Chinese, Dutch, and Native American heritage, Woods brought millions of new fans to golf and inspired a generation of players from underrepresented backgrounds. PGA Tour television ratings during his peak were routinely double or triple what they were in his absence. Prize money on the PGA Tour tripled during his prime years.
